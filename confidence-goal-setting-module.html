Lesson 1

The Confidence Question

What is confidence, where does it come from, and why do some people seem to have more of it than others?

SHAPE America Standard 5 SEL: Self-Awareness IL Goal 19

Think About This

Picture two students in PE class. The teacher introduces a new skill — let's say dribbling a basketball with their non-dominant hand.

Student A tries it right away, messes up a few times, laughs it off, and keeps practicing. By the end of class, they're getting the hang of it.

Student B watches everyone else first, tries once, loses control of the ball, and says "I can't do this." They stop trying.

Same skill. Same teacher. Same class. What's different?

The answer isn't talent. It's confidence.

Specifically, it's something psychologists call self-efficacy — your belief in your ability to succeed at a specific task. And here's the most important thing about it: confidence isn't something you're born with. It's built.

The Four Sources of Confidence

Psychologist Albert Bandura identified four ways people build (or lose) confidence:

The single strongest source of confidence. When you successfully complete a challenge, your brain records it as evidence that you're capable. The more small wins you stack, the more you believe in yourself.

PE Example: A student who makes 3 out of 5 free throws starts to believe they can actually shoot. That belief makes them try harder, practice more, and improve faster.

Watching someone similar to you succeed gives you evidence that it's possible. This is why seeing a classmate (not just the star athlete) nail a skill is so powerful.

PE Example: A student who thinks they "can't serve" watches another kid who used to struggle land three serves in a row. Suddenly it feels possible.

Encouragement from a coach, teacher, or peer can boost confidence — but only if it's specific and genuine. "Good job" means less than "Your follow-through was way better that time."

PE Example: A teacher says "You kept your eyes up while dribbling — that's exactly what we practiced." The student now knows specifically what they did right.

Anxiety, stress, and frustration can tank confidence. A calm, focused, positive emotional state helps you believe you can succeed. This is why warm-ups and positive environments matter.

PE Example: A student who feels nervous about being watched during a fitness test performs worse than they do in practice. Their body is telling their brain "this is dangerous," even though it isn't.

Lesson 2

Goals That Actually Work

Most goals fail — not because people are lazy, but because the goal itself was broken from the start.

SHAPE America Standard 4 SEL: Self-Management IL Goal 20

The Problem With Most Goals

How many times have you heard (or said) something like this?

  • "I want to get better at basketball."
  • "I'm going to run faster."
  • "I want to be more fit."

These feel like goals, but they're really just wishes. There's no target. No way to know if you've actually made progress. And when you can't measure progress, you can't experience the small wins that build confidence.

Wish vs. Goal

A wish is something you hope happens. A goal is something you can plan for, measure, and achieve. The difference between them is structure.

What Makes a Goal Work?

Researchers Edwin Locke and Gary Latham spent decades studying goal setting. They found that goals work best when they are:

Specific

Say exactly what you want to accomplish. Not "get better at basketball" but "make 7 out of 10 free throws."

Ask yourself: Could someone else read this goal and know exactly what I'm trying to do?

Measurable

Attach a number to it. If you can't measure it, you can't track it. Numbers give you proof of progress.

Ask yourself: How will I know when I've achieved this? What number tells me I'm there?

Achievable

The goal should stretch you but not break you. If you currently make 2 out of 10 free throws, aiming for 10 out of 10 by next week sets you up to fail. Aim for 4 or 5 first.

Ask yourself: Is this challenging but realistic given where I am right now?

Relevant

The goal should matter to YOU. Not your teacher, not your parents — you. When you care about the goal, you'll put in the effort.

Ask yourself: Why does this goal matter to me? What will it feel like when I achieve it?

Time-Bound

Give it a deadline. "Someday" is not a plan. A deadline creates urgency and gives you a window to measure progress.

Ask yourself: When do I want to achieve this by? What's my checkpoint?

Goal Makeover

Let's transform some vague wishes into structured goals. For each wish below, write a goal that is specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound.

Wish: "I want to run faster."

Wish: "I want to be better at volleyball."

Wish: "I want to be more fit."

Lesson 3

The Power of Small Wins

You don't build confidence by doing something incredible once. You build it by stacking small successes — over and over.

SHAPE America Standard 4 SHAPE America Standard 5 SEL: Self-Management IL Goal 19
Scenario: Marcus and the Mile

Marcus is in 7th grade PE. His class is starting a running unit, and the first challenge is the one-mile run.

Marcus hasn't run a full mile before. Last year, he walked most of it and finished last. He's already telling himself, "I'm just not a runner."

His teacher doesn't ask him to run a mile on day one. Instead, here's what happens:

Day 1: Run to the first cone and walk back

That's it. About 100 yards. Marcus does it easily. "OK, that wasn't bad."

Day 3: Run one full lap without stopping

Marcus finishes the lap. He's breathing hard but he did it. Small win logged.

Day 5: Run two laps, walk one, run one more

He's doing more than he thought he could. His internal voice shifts from "I can't" to "maybe I can."

Day 8: Run the full mile

Marcus finishes. Not fast. Not pretty. But he finishes. And for the first time, he believes he's a runner.

What happened here?

Marcus's teacher used structured goal setting to create a series of mastery experiences. Each step was achievable. Each success built evidence. By the time Marcus attempted the full mile, he had already proven to himself — four times — that he could do hard things.

The Scaffolding Formula

This approach has a name in education: scaffolding. And it follows a simple pattern:

Start Simple → Build Success → Increase Challenge → Confidence Grows

Break It Down

Take a big goal and break it into 3-4 smaller steps that build on each other. Start with something you know you can do. Each step should be slightly harder than the last.

Lesson 4

When Things Don't Go As Planned

Setbacks happen to everyone. What separates confident people from everyone else isn't avoiding failure — it's how they talk to themselves about it.

SHAPE America Standard 4 SEL: Self-Awareness SEL: Self-Management IL Goal 21
Scenario: Aaliyah's Setback

Aaliyah set a goal to complete 15 curl-ups during the fitness test. She's been practicing and hit 12 last week — a personal best.

Test day arrives. She's nervous. Her stomach is tight. She starts strong but loses her rhythm at rep 9 and stops at 11.

She didn't hit her goal. Now two voices in her head start talking:

The Destructive Voice

"I knew I'd mess up. I'm terrible at this. Everyone else did better than me. Why do I even try?"

The Productive Voice

"11 is still more than I could do a month ago. I got nervous and lost my rhythm — I can work on that. I'll hit 15 next time."

Same situation. Two completely different outcomes.

The destructive voice treats the setback as proof of failure. The productive voice treats it as data — information about what to adjust next time. This is called self-talk, and it's one of the most powerful tools in sports psychology.

The Reframe

Productive self-talk doesn't mean being fake positive. It means being accurate. Most negative self-talk is actually an exaggeration. Let's practice reframing:

"I can't do this." →
Hint: Add one word that changes everything.
"Everyone is better than me." →
Hint: Are you really comparing fairly?
"I always mess up." →
Hint: Is "always" actually true?
"I'm just not athletic." →
Hint: Is this a permanent truth or a current state?

The Setback Formula

When something doesn't go as planned, use these three steps:

1

Acknowledge It

"That didn't go the way I wanted."

2

Analyze It

"What specifically went wrong? What can I adjust?"

3

Adjust It

"Here's what I'll do differently next time."
