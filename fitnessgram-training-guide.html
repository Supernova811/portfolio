Section 1

Understanding FitnessGram

Before administering a single test, every educator needs to understand what FitnessGram measures, why standardized protocols exist, and how the data is used to support student health.

What Is FitnessGram?

FitnessGram is the most widely used youth fitness assessment in the United States. Developed by The Cooper Institute, it provides a standardized battery of tests that measure health-related fitness components — not athletic ability.

The purpose is not to rank students against each other. It is to compare each student's results against research-based health standards called Healthy Fitness Zones.

The 5 Test Components

FitnessGram assesses five areas of health-related fitness:

▶ PACER — Aerobic Capacity ▶ Curl-Up — Abdominal Strength ▶ Trunk Lift — Trunk Extensor Strength/Flexibility ▶ Push-Up — Upper Body Strength ▶ Sit & Reach — Flexibility

Each test has a specific protocol that must be followed exactly. When everyone administers the test the same way, the data is valid and comparable year to year.

Healthy Fitness Zone (HFZ)

FitnessGram scores are not graded on a curve. Instead, each score falls into one of three categories based on the student's age and gender:

HFZ — Healthy Fitness Zone NI-HFZ — Needs Improvement NI-HFZ Health Risk — Needs Improvement (Health Risk)

These zones are based on research linking fitness levels to health outcomes. A student who scores in the HFZ is meeting the minimum threshold associated with good health for their age group.

Key Concept
HFZ thresholds differ by age and gender. A 10-year-old boy and a 14-year-old girl have different cut-off scores for the same test. Always reference the correct standards table.

Why Consistency Matters

FitnessGram data is only useful when every administrator follows the same protocol, every time. Consider what happens when protocols are inconsistent:

  • Unreliable data — Scores cannot be compared across classes, schools, or years
  • Bad decisions — A student may appear to improve or decline based on how the test was run, not their actual fitness
  • Lost credibility — Parents, administrators, and other stakeholders can't trust the results
  • Wasted time — If data can't be used, the testing period served no purpose
Bottom Line
Inconsistent protocols = unreliable data = bad decisions. Standardization is not optional.

Illinois State Mandate

FitnessGram is not optional in Illinois. As of the 2025-2026 school year, FitnessGram is the state-mandated fitness assessment for all Illinois public schools under the Enhanced Physical Education initiative.

  • Legal authority: 105 ILCS 5/27-720 (fitness assessment rules)
  • Curriculum standards: 105 ILCS 5/27-725
  • Administrative code: Illinois Administrative Code Section 1.425

Schools are required to submit FitnessGram data to the state through the IWAS (ISBE Web Application Security) system. This means your administration protocols directly affect state-level reporting — accuracy matters.

Illinois PE aligns to six state goals (Goals 19-24), covering motor skills, fitness, teamwork, health promotion, human body systems, and health communication. FitnessGram assessment supports Goals 19 and 20 directly.

Source
Illinois State Board of Education — Enhanced Physical Education

Section 2

Test Administration Protocols

Expand each test below to review the full administration protocol, step by step. Pay close attention to the common mistakes — these are where data reliability breaks down.

Setup

  • Mark a 20-meter course with two lines (cones recommended at each end)
  • Use the official PACER audio CD or app for cadence
  • Students line up at one end of the course

Protocol

  • Students run back and forth across the 20-meter distance, keeping pace with an audio beep
  • The first lap is slow (approximately 9 seconds) and gets progressively faster
  • Students must reach the line BEFORE the beep sounds
  • A student is allowed one missed beep — they are out after missing two beeps total (they do not need to be consecutive)
  • Record the total number of laps completed

Key Rules

  • Students must touch or cross the line on each lap — no cutting corners
  • Students should not start before the audio signals the beginning
  • Encourage effort but do not coach pace during the test
Common Mistakes: Letting students start late or before the signal. Allowing students to cut corners and not reach the line. Counting a lap when the student clearly missed the beep.

Setup

  • Student lies on their back on a mat, knees bent at approximately 140 degrees, feet flat on the floor
  • Arms straight at sides, fingers touching the near edge of a measuring strip
  • Strip width: 3 inches (ages 5–9) or 4.5 inches (ages 10+)

Protocol

  • Student curls up slowly, sliding fingers across the strip until they touch the far edge
  • Performed to a cadence of 1 curl-up every 3 seconds (use official audio)
  • Heels must remain in contact with the floor
  • Test ends when the student can no longer maintain the cadence, or reaches the maximum of 75 curl-ups

Key Rules

  • Head does not need to touch the floor between reps, but shoulder blades must
  • Movement should be smooth and controlled, not jerky
  • Student should breathe normally throughout
Common Mistakes: Students pulling their head forward with their hands (arms must stay at sides). Feet lifting off the floor. Not using the cadence audio and allowing students to rush.

Setup

  • Student lies face down on a mat
  • Hands are placed under the thighs
  • Place a coin or marker on the floor in front of the student's eyes as a focus point

Protocol

  • Student lifts their upper body off the floor in a slow, controlled manner
  • They hold the position while you measure the distance from the floor to the chin
  • Maximum score is 12 inches — do NOT record anything higher
  • Allow two trials and record the higher score

Key Rules

  • Student must look at the focus point to keep head in a neutral position
  • The lift should be slow and controlled, not a quick jerk
  • Hands must remain under the thighs at all times
Common Mistakes: Recording measurements above 12 inches (scores higher than 12 indicate hyperextension, which is unsafe and should not be encouraged). Letting students use their arms to push up. Not capping at 12 inches.

Setup

  • Student assumes a standard push-up position: hands under or slightly wider than shoulders, body straight from head to toes
  • A partner or the tester observes form

Protocol

  • Performed to a cadence of 1 push-up every 3 seconds (use official audio)
  • Student lowers body until elbows reach a 90-degree angle, then pushes back up
  • Test ends when student can no longer maintain cadence or form breaks down
  • Record the total number of push-ups completed with correct form

Key Rules

  • Body must remain straight throughout — no sagging or piking
  • Elbows must reach 90 degrees on every rep to count
  • Only count reps completed with proper form
Common Mistakes: Sagging hips (most common form breakdown). Not going low enough — many students only bend slightly. Counting reps after form has broken.

Setup

  • Use a standard sit-and-reach box
  • Student removes shoes
  • Student sits on the floor with one leg extended against the box and the other knee bent, foot flat on the floor, to the side of the straight leg

Protocol

  • Student places one hand on top of the other, palms down
  • Student reaches forward slowly along the top of the box with both hands
  • Record the distance reached in inches or centimeters
  • Measure BOTH the left and right sides separately
  • Allow practice reaches first, then record the best of multiple attempts per side

Key Rules

  • The extended knee must remain straight (locked) during the measurement
  • The reach must be a slow, sustained movement — no bouncing
  • Keep the back as flat as possible during the reach
Common Mistakes: Allowing students to bounce forward to reach farther. Not keeping the extended knee straight (bending invalidates the measurement). Forgetting to test both left and right sides.

Section 3

Common Scenarios & Decision Points

Testing rarely goes perfectly. Read each scenario, decide what you would do, and check your answer. These are the judgment calls that separate reliable data from guesswork.

Scenario 1 — PACER
A student reaches the line just as the beep sounds on the PACER. Do they continue running?
Correct. The rule is students must reach the line by the beep. Arriving exactly on the beep satisfies this requirement. The student continues.
Not quite. The standard is that students must reach the line by the beep. Arriving on the beep counts — they only miss if they are short of the line when the beep sounds.
Scenario 2 — Curl-Up
During curl-ups, you notice a student is pulling their head forward with their hands instead of keeping arms at their sides. What do you do?
Correct. Improper form means the test is not measuring what it's supposed to measure. Stop the student, demonstrate the correct technique (arms at sides, curl from the core), and let them restart. Data integrity matters more than finishing fast.
Incorrect form invalidates the data. The right move is to stop the student, demonstrate proper technique (arms at sides, curl from the core), and have them restart. Never record scores from improper form.
Scenario 3 — Trunk Lift
A student's trunk lift measurement reads 14 inches. What do you record?
Correct. The maximum trunk lift score is 12 inches. Scores above 12 indicate hyperextension, which is unsafe and should not be encouraged or rewarded. Always cap at 12.
The correct answer is 12 inches. Trunk lift is capped at 12 inches because anything above that indicates hyperextension, which poses an injury risk. Never record above the maximum.
Scenario 4 — PACER
A student asks to redo the PACER because they "weren't ready." Do you allow a retest?
Correct. Allowing immediate retests undermines standardization and creates fairness issues. Students get one attempt per testing period. If a legitimate reason for a retest exists, it should be scheduled for a different day.
The standard is one attempt per testing period. Immediate retests compromise data consistency and fairness. If a retest is warranted, schedule it for a different testing day.
Scenario 5 — Push-Up
During push-ups, a student's hips start sagging on rep 8. They've pushed through and completed what they say are 8 reps. What do you record?
Correct. You record the last rep completed with correct form. If form broke on rep 8, the score is 7. A rep only counts when performed with proper technique — straight body, elbows to 90 degrees, no sagging.
The correct answer is 7. Only reps completed with proper form count. If form broke at rep 8, the last valid rep was 7. Notes don't change the score — form is the standard.
Reflection
Every one of these scenarios comes up during real testing. Knowing the answers in advance means you won't second-guess yourself in the moment — and your data will be reliable.
